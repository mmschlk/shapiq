# Grid-search exploration: where does multi-output ProxySHAP break?

Generated by `grid_search.py`. Fixed knobs: `n_features=12`, `index=SII`,
`max_order=2`, fit `budget=256` coalitions, held-out `eval_budget=256` *fresh*
coalitions, `random_state=0`. `make_classification` needs
`n_classes * n_clusters_per_class <= 2**n_informative`; with
`n_clusters_per_class=1` we set `n_informative = clip(ceil(log2(c)), 2, 12)`
and `n_samples = max(600, 3*c)` (a few rows per class).

## Study 1 — Scale c toward 1000 (`grid_c_scaling.csv`, `grid_c_scaling.png`)

**Nothing broke.** Every `c` in {10, 50, 100, 250, 500, 1000} completed with
status `ok` — no exception, no `MemoryError`, no pathological slowdown.

| c | proxy fit (s) | fused (ms) | naive (ms) | speedup | dense entries |
|------|------|------|------|------|------|
| 10   | 0.016 | 7.2  | 10.9  | 1.52x | 790 |
| 100  | 0.087 | 47.7 | 80.2  | 1.68x | 7 900 |
| 1000 | 0.824 | 497.6 | 763.6 | 1.53x | 79 000 |

Everything scales **linearly and benignly** in `c`: fit time, fused time, naive
time and the dense result size (`c * sum_{k<=2} C(12,k) = c*79`) all grow ~10x
from c=10 to c=1000. The fused-vs-naive speedup stays flat at ~1.5–1.7x — it
does **not** grow with `c` (consistent with the canonical benchmark: the fused
path still does O(c) work outside the amortized preprocessing). At c=1000 the
multi-output proxy fits in under one second and explains in half a second; the
real approach does not break on raw scale.

## Study 2 — Fit quality vs c: multi_output_tree vs one_output_per_tree (`grid_fit_quality.csv`, `grid_fit_quality.png`)

This is where the approach actually deteriorates. Both XGBoost multi-strategies
were fit on the **same** 256 training coalitions and scored on 256 **fresh**
held-out coalitions (R^2 averaged over outputs; worst-output R^2 also reported).

| c | mot R²_mean | mot R²_worst | opt R²_mean | opt R²_worst | gap (opt−mot) |
|------|------|------|------|------|------|
| 10   | 0.952 | 0.834 | 0.972 | 0.910 | 0.020 |
| 50   | 0.826 | 0.578 | 0.893 | 0.715 | 0.067 |
| 100  | 0.785 | 0.427 | 0.859 | 0.556 | 0.074 |
| 250  | 0.645 | 0.236 | 0.824 | 0.448 | 0.179 |
| 500  | 0.605 | −0.568 | 0.767 | −0.018 | 0.162 |
| 1000 | 0.575 | −1.044 | 0.672 | −0.334 | 0.096 |

**Headline:** the fusable `multi_output_tree` proxy's mean held-out R^2 falls
from **0.952 at c=10 to 0.575 at c=1000** — it loses roughly 40 points of R^2.
The worst-output R^2 collapses far harder: from 0.83 at c=10 to **−1.04 at
c=1000** (worse than predicting the mean). So at high `c` the shared-topology
proxy is *actively bad* for its hardest outputs.

The shared-topology penalty is real and grows with `c`: the gap to
`one_output_per_tree` widens from 0.02 (c=10) to a peak of **0.18 at c=250**
before both strategies degrade together at very high `c` (the gap shrinks to
0.10 at c=1000 only because `opt` is also failing — its mean R^2 is 0.67).
Forcing all `c` outputs through one set of splits is the genuine limiting
factor of the fused approach: once `c` is in the hundreds, the proxy you can
fuse is a substantially worse approximation of the value function than the
`c`-independent-tree proxy you cannot fuse. The fused kernel works fine; the
*model it explains* is what degrades.

## Study 3 — Hyperparameter grid at c=50 (`grid_xgb_params.csv`, `grid_xgb_params.png`)

`max_depth` in {3,6,10} x `n_estimators` in {10,50}, `multi_output_tree`, c=50.

| depth | n_est | fit (s) | R²_mean | fused ms (pre/ker) | naive ms | speedup |
|----|----|------|------|------|------|------|
| 3  | 10 | 0.02 | 0.718 | 13.8 (5.1/8.7)   | 27.8  | 2.01x |
| 3  | 50 | 0.10 | 0.867 | 21.3 (14.6/6.8)  | 104.5 | 4.90x |
| 6  | 10 | 0.05 | 0.826 | 24.8 (18.0/6.8)  | 36.7  | 1.48x |
| 6  | 50 | 0.23 | 0.891 | 106.2 (98.8/7.4) | 156.2 | 1.47x |
| 10 | 10 | 0.14 | 0.828 | 40.1 (33.2/6.9)  | 53.7  | 1.34x |
| 10 | 50 | 0.39 | 0.864 | 164.2 (155.4/8.7)| 212.1 | 1.29x |

The depth/quality/speedup picture:

- **Quality.** `n_estimators` helps more than `max_depth`. Going 10→50 trees
  lifts R^2 substantially at every depth (e.g. depth 3: 0.718→0.867). Going
  deeper has diminishing/negative returns: depth 6 ≈ depth 10, and depth 10
  with 50 trees (0.864) is actually slightly *worse* than depth 6 with 50
  trees (0.891) — deep multi-output trees overfit the shared topology.

- **Speedup vs depth — refuted.** The hypothesis "deeper trees = more
  amortizable structural work = fused pulls further ahead" is **not** borne
  out. Speedup *falls* with depth: 2.0x→1.5x→1.3x at depth 3/6/10 (n_est=10).
  The reason is in the pre/ker split: the fused kernel time stays ~7–9 ms
  flat, but the per-tree **preprocessing** (the E/R partition DFS) is itself
  O(tree size) and dominates the fused total at depth 10 (155 of 164 ms). The
  fused path runs that DFS once per tree but the naive path runs it `c` times
  per tree — yet preprocessing grows fast enough with depth that the *constant*
  c-amortization is swamped. The clearest win is shallow + many trees
  (depth 3, 50 trees: 4.9x), where preprocessing is cheap and the c-fold
  amortization dominates.

## Takeaways

1. The fused kernel and the multi-output pipeline do **not** crash or blow up
   at scale — c=1000 runs in ~1.3 s end-to-end with linear, benign scaling.
2. The genuine limiting factor is **proxy fit quality**: `multi_output_tree`
   held-out R^2 drops from 0.95 (c=10) to 0.58 (c=1000), with worst-output R^2
   going negative beyond c~250. The shared-topology penalty vs
   `one_output_per_tree` peaks at ~0.18 R^2 around c=250.
3. For a usable fused proxy, prefer **shallow trees with more boosting rounds**:
   it both fits better (less overfitting) and yields the largest fused speedup
   (~5x at depth 3 / 50 trees), since deep trees make per-tree preprocessing
   the bottleneck.
