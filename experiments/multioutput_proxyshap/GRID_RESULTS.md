# Grid-search exploration: where does multi-output ProxySHAP break?

Generated by `grid_search.py`. Fixed knobs: `n_features=12`, `index=SII`,
`max_order=2`, fit `budget=256` coalitions, held-out `eval_budget=256` *fresh*
coalitions, `random_state=0`. `make_classification` needs
`n_classes * n_clusters_per_class <= 2**n_informative`; with
`n_clusters_per_class=1` we set `n_informative = clip(ceil(log2(c)), 2, 12)`
and `n_samples = max(600, 3*c)` (a few rows per class).

## Study 1 ‚ÄĒ Scale c toward 1000 (`grid_c_scaling.csv`, `grid_c_scaling.png`)

**Nothing broke.** Every `c` in {10, 50, 100, 250, 500, 1000} completed with
status `ok` ‚ÄĒ no exception, no `MemoryError`, no pathological slowdown.

| c | proxy fit (s) | fused (ms) | naive (ms) | speedup | dense entries |
|------|------|------|------|------|------|
| 10   | 0.016 | 7.2  | 10.9  | 1.52x | 790 |
| 100  | 0.087 | 47.7 | 80.2  | 1.68x | 7 900 |
| 1000 | 0.824 | 497.6 | 763.6 | 1.53x | 79 000 |

Everything scales **linearly and benignly** in `c`: fit time, fused time, naive
time and the dense result size (`c * sum_{k<=2} C(12,k) = c*79`) all grow ~10x
from c=10 to c=1000. The fused-vs-naive speedup stays flat at ~1.5‚Äď1.7x ‚ÄĒ it
does **not** grow with `c` (consistent with the canonical benchmark: the fused
path still does O(c) work outside the amortized preprocessing). At c=1000 the
multi-output proxy fits in under one second and explains in half a second; the
real approach does not break on raw scale.

## Study 2 ‚ÄĒ Fit quality vs c: multi_output_tree vs one_output_per_tree (`grid_fit_quality.csv`, `grid_fit_quality.png`)

This is where the approach actually deteriorates. Both XGBoost multi-strategies
were fit on the **same** 256 training coalitions and scored on 256 **fresh**
held-out coalitions (R^2 averaged over outputs; worst-output R^2 also reported).

| c | mot R¬≤_mean | mot R¬≤_worst | opt R¬≤_mean | opt R¬≤_worst | gap (opt‚ąímot) | multi fit (s) | oneper fit (s) | fit ratio |
|------|------|------|------|------|------|------|------|------|
| 10   | 0.952 | 0.834 | 0.972 | 0.910 | 0.020 | 0.015 | 0.024 | 1.61x |
| 50   | 0.826 | 0.578 | 0.893 | 0.715 | 0.067 | 0.049 | 0.103 | 2.12x |
| 100  | 0.785 | 0.427 | 0.859 | 0.556 | 0.074 | 0.089 | 0.197 | 2.21x |
| 250  | 0.645 | 0.236 | 0.824 | 0.448 | 0.179 | 0.212 | 0.434 | 2.05x |
| 500  | 0.605 | ‚ąí0.568 | 0.767 | ‚ąí0.018 | 0.162 | 0.447 | 3.167 | 7.08x* |
| 1000 | 0.575 | ‚ąí1.044 | 0.672 | ‚ąí0.334 | 0.096 | 0.835 | 1.080 | 1.29x |

`multi fit` is the `multi_output_tree` `.fit()` wall-clock; `oneper fit` is the
`one_output_per_tree` `.fit()` wall-clock; `fit ratio` is `oneper / multi`. The
estimator object is constructed *outside* the timed region so the wall-clock
covers only `.fit()`. (*The c=500 `oneper` time is an outlier measurement spike
‚ÄĒ 3.17 s where the linear trend predicts ~0.9 s; c=1000 is back on trend at
1.08 s. See the subsection below.)

**Headline:** the fusable `multi_output_tree` proxy's mean held-out R^2 falls
from **0.952 at c=10 to 0.575 at c=1000** ‚ÄĒ it loses roughly 40 points of R^2.
The worst-output R^2 collapses far harder: from 0.83 at c=10 to **‚ąí1.04 at
c=1000** (worse than predicting the mean). So at high `c` the shared-topology
proxy is *actively bad* for its hardest outputs.

The shared-topology penalty is real and grows with `c`: the gap to
`one_output_per_tree` widens from 0.02 (c=10) to a peak of **0.18 at c=250**
before both strategies degrade together at very high `c` (the gap shrinks to
0.10 at c=1000 only because `opt` is also failing ‚ÄĒ its mean R^2 is 0.67).
Forcing all `c` outputs through one set of splits is the genuine limiting
factor of the fused approach: once `c` is in the hundreds, the proxy you can
fuse is a substantially worse approximation of the value function than the
`c`-independent-tree proxy you cannot fuse. The fused kernel works fine; the
*model it explains* is what degrades.

### Fitting once vs c times

Quality is one axis; **fit cost** is a separate one. Explaining a multivariate
value function *today* means fitting `c` separate scalar proxies ‚ÄĒ one per
output. `MultiOutputProxySHAP` instead fits **one** `multi_output_tree`. The
right stand-in for "fit `c` times" is `one_output_per_tree` fitting a
`c`-column target: it grows the boosted ensemble one output at a time, so its
`.fit()` wall-clock is the honest cost of the `c`-scalar-proxy status quo.
`multi_output_tree` is the single fused proxy our approach fits. The ratio
`fit_time_oneper_s / fit_time_multi_s` (logged per `c` in
`grid_fit_quality.csv`) therefore quantifies a real end-to-end speedup source
that is **separate** from the kernel/explain ~2x.

Across the `c` sweep the ratio is **1.6x ‚Üí 2.1x ‚Üí 2.2x ‚Üí 2.0x** for
c=10/50/100/250, an apparent **7.1x** at c=500 and **1.3x** at c=1000. The
c=500 point is a measurement spike (the `oneper` fit clocks 3.17 s where the
linear trend of every other point predicts ~0.9 s ‚ÄĒ the c=1000 `oneper` fit is
1.08 s, *less* than the spurious c=500 value); ignoring it, fitting one
`multi_output_tree` is consistently **~1.3‚Äď2.2x cheaper** than fitting the
`c`-column `one_output_per_tree`, settling around ~1.3x at the largest `c`.

So yes ‚ÄĒ fitting one `multi_output_tree` *is* cheaper than `one_output_per_tree`,
but it is **not** a free `c`x win, and it should not be expected to be. The
`multi_output_tree` split-gain computation is internally `c`-wide: every
candidate split still evaluates the loss reduction over all `c` output columns,
so the dominant per-split cost scales with `c` just as it does for
`one_output_per_tree`. What fusing the topology saves is the *structural*
overhead ‚ÄĒ one shared split search, one tree to grow and store, one set of
node-bookkeeping passes ‚ÄĒ not the `c`-wide gradient/hessian arithmetic. That
overhead is a roughly constant-factor (~1.3‚Äď2.2x), not order-`c`, saving, and it
shrinks toward ~1.3x at c=1000 as the `c`-wide arithmetic dominates both
strategies.

**Honest end-to-end picture.** The multi-output ProxySHAP approach has two
distinct, multiplicative speedup sources, both modest constant factors:

- **Fit side:** ~1.3‚Äď2.2x from fitting one fused `multi_output_tree` instead of
  the `c`-column `one_output_per_tree` proxy (this subsection).
- **Explain side:** ~1.5‚Äď2x from the fused multi-output kernel vs the naive
  per-output scalar-explainer loop (Study 1; up to ~5x for shallow + many-tree
  proxies in Study 3).

Combined, the realistic end-to-end win is in the **~2‚Äď4x** range for typical
configs (e.g. ~2x fit x ~2x explain), reaching higher only for shallow proxies
where the explain-side speedup peaks. It is emphatically **not** an order-`c`
speedup: both the fused fit and the fused kernel still do O(`c`) arithmetic
work, and what is amortized away is per-tree structural overhead, not the
`c`-wide core computation. This must be weighed against the Study 2 quality
penalty ‚ÄĒ the shared-topology `multi_output_tree` is a measurably worse proxy
once `c` reaches the hundreds.

## Study 3 ‚ÄĒ Hyperparameter grid at c=50 (`grid_xgb_params.csv`, `grid_xgb_params.png`)

`max_depth` in {3,6,10} x `n_estimators` in {10,50}, `multi_output_tree`, c=50.

| depth | n_est | fit (s) | R¬≤_mean | fused ms (pre/ker) | naive ms | speedup |
|----|----|------|------|------|------|------|
| 3  | 10 | 0.02 | 0.718 | 13.8 (5.1/8.7)   | 27.8  | 2.01x |
| 3  | 50 | 0.10 | 0.867 | 21.3 (14.6/6.8)  | 104.5 | 4.90x |
| 6  | 10 | 0.05 | 0.826 | 24.8 (18.0/6.8)  | 36.7  | 1.48x |
| 6  | 50 | 0.23 | 0.891 | 106.2 (98.8/7.4) | 156.2 | 1.47x |
| 10 | 10 | 0.14 | 0.828 | 40.1 (33.2/6.9)  | 53.7  | 1.34x |
| 10 | 50 | 0.39 | 0.864 | 164.2 (155.4/8.7)| 212.1 | 1.29x |

The depth/quality/speedup picture:

- **Quality.** `n_estimators` helps more than `max_depth`. Going 10‚Üí50 trees
  lifts R^2 substantially at every depth (e.g. depth 3: 0.718‚Üí0.867). Going
  deeper has diminishing/negative returns: depth 6 ‚Čą depth 10, and depth 10
  with 50 trees (0.864) is actually slightly *worse* than depth 6 with 50
  trees (0.891) ‚ÄĒ deep multi-output trees overfit the shared topology.

- **Speedup vs depth ‚ÄĒ refuted.** The hypothesis "deeper trees = more
  amortizable structural work = fused pulls further ahead" is **not** borne
  out. Speedup *falls* with depth: 2.0x‚Üí1.5x‚Üí1.3x at depth 3/6/10 (n_est=10).
  The reason is in the pre/ker split: the fused kernel time stays ~7‚Äď9 ms
  flat, but the per-tree **preprocessing** (the E/R partition DFS) is itself
  O(tree size) and dominates the fused total at depth 10 (155 of 164 ms). The
  fused path runs that DFS once per tree but the naive path runs it `c` times
  per tree ‚ÄĒ yet preprocessing grows fast enough with depth that the *constant*
  c-amortization is swamped. The clearest win is shallow + many trees
  (depth 3, 50 trees: 4.9x), where preprocessing is cheap and the c-fold
  amortization dominates.

## Takeaways

1. The fused kernel and the multi-output pipeline do **not** crash or blow up
   at scale ‚ÄĒ c=1000 runs in ~1.3 s end-to-end with linear, benign scaling.
2. The genuine limiting factor is **proxy fit quality**: `multi_output_tree`
   held-out R^2 drops from 0.95 (c=10) to 0.58 (c=1000), with worst-output R^2
   going negative beyond c~250. The shared-topology penalty vs
   `one_output_per_tree` peaks at ~0.18 R^2 around c=250.
3. For a usable fused proxy, prefer **shallow trees with more boosting rounds**:
   it both fits better (less overfitting) and yields the largest fused speedup
   (~5x at depth 3 / 50 trees), since deep trees make per-tree preprocessing
   the bottleneck.
4. The end-to-end win has **two** modest constant-factor sources: ~1.3‚Äď2.2x on
   the **fit** side (one fused `multi_output_tree` vs the `c`-column
   `one_output_per_tree`) and ~1.5‚Äď2x on the **explain** side (fused kernel vs
   naive loop) ‚ÄĒ realistically **~2‚Äď4x** combined, *not* an order-`c` speedup,
   because both stages still do O(`c`) arithmetic and only per-tree structural
   overhead is amortized away.
