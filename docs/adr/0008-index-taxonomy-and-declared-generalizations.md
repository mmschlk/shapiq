# Index Taxonomy And Declared Generalizations

The capability protocols carry the names of the cooperative-game-theory concepts they encode. The discrete-derivative capability is the cardinal interaction index of Grabisch and Roubens — weights depending only on the cardinalities of the interaction and the outside coalition — and was renamed from its descriptive working name accordingly; SV, BV, SII, BII, the chaining index CHII, STII, and the Moebius and Co-Moebius transforms provide it. A second capability covers the generalized values, whose attributions weight bloc marginal contributions ``v(T | S) - v(T)`` instead of discrete derivatives; SGV, BGV, CHGV, the internal and external values IGV and EGV, and JointSV provide it, and notably the Shapley, Banzhaf, and chaining generalized values reuse the exact weight profiles of their interaction-index cousins — the taxonomy makes that sharing literal. The transforms are ordinary cardinal indices with degenerate weight profiles anchored at the empty coalition or the complement, an order of ``None`` meaning all orders (resolved to the player count at the explainer), and kernel-computed order-0 attributions, which is why Co-Moebius carries ``v(N)`` rather than ``v(empty)`` at order zero.

Three indices keep dedicated exact solvers rather than a shared capability, deliberately: k-SII is an aggregation of SII via Bernoulli numbers, not a kernel of its own; FBII is an unconstrained uniform-kernel fit with a free intercept, which contradicts the constrained-interpolation contract of the regression capability (its order-0 attribution is the fitted intercept, not the empty value); and kADD-SHAP fits in a Bernoulli-weighted basis where the grand-coalition constraint is a general design row, eliminated exactly by pivoting instead of the all-ones substitution the membership basis allows. Inventing a capability wide enough for all three would have made the protocol describe "some least squares problem", which selects nothing. kADD-SHAP still declares the Shapley regression kernel, so a future kernel-matched sampled estimator can accept it.

Interaction indices declare the probabilistic value their order-1 restriction equals: SII, CHII, STII, k-SII, FSII, kADD-SHAP, SGV, CHGV, and JointSV generalize the Shapley value; BII, FBII, and BGV generalize the Banzhaf value; the values themselves, the transforms, and the internal and external generalized values declare none. The declaration is index metadata (`generalizes`), not an equality of objects: ``SII(order=1)`` and ``SV()`` remain distinct — they dispatch to different estimators and label explanations differently — and object equality across index types was rejected because it would corrupt type-keyed dispatch and hashing while still not making explanations interchangeable at higher orders. What makes the declaration trustworthy is that it is executable: a parametrized test constructs every declaring index at order one, explains the same game with it and with its declared value, and requires the attributions to agree, so a wrong claim fails the suite rather than surviving as documentation. This test made the chaining identity — CHII at order one is exactly SV, since ``(1+t)C(n,1+t) = nC(n-1,t)`` — a checked fact rather than folklore.

Exact computation for every family shares one dispatch in the exact explainer, ordered dedicated solvers first, then cardinal, generalized-value, and constrained-regression capabilities; the ordering matters because SV intentionally satisfies both the cardinal and regression capabilities and must take the cheaper derivative path, and kADD-SHAP satisfies the regression capability but needs its own basis. Sampled estimators are unchanged: the permutation and Shapley-kernel regression entry points keep their closed index sets, and extending them to the new families is future work per family, not a side effect of this port.
